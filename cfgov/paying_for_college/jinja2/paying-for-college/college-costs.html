Your financial path to graduation

Have a financial aid offer? We’ll help you plan to finish your program with debt you can afford.

This tool can help you:

  • Understand your financial aid offer
  • Plan to cover the remaining costs
  • Estimate how much you’ll owe and if you can afford that debt
  • Compare offers from different schools
  • Decide what to do next

Keep coming back as your plan evolves.

For many students, planning how to pay for school is complicated. It may require multiple conversations with family, advisers, and the school financial aid office to get more information. As you figure out your best options, come back and update your plan as many times as you need! (This tool does not save your personal data. Read more about how this tool protects your privacy.)

To start, you will need:

  • Your financial aid offer(s)
  • The phone number or email address of someone in the financial aid office—it’s their job to answer your questions!
  • An idea of how much money you and your family can put towards school—it’s okay if it’s $0!
  • A private loan offer, if you have one
  • You may want to use this tool with someone you trust who can help you weigh your options.

    Using student loans strategically

    Next you will make a plan to cover remaining costs. Since you will probably use loans to pay for school—most students do—it’s important to understand how they work. This page will overview how student loans work and their potential benefits and risks.

    Reducing the cost of student loans

    Student loans are money for education that you pay back with interest. That means you'll pay more than you originally borrowed.

    For example, if you borrow $10,000 in unsubsidized loans with 4% annual interest, you will accrue $400 in interest each year. Suppose you borrow that $10,000 in September 2020 and graduate in May 2024. When you enter repayment 6 months later, in November 2024, you’ll owe a total of $11,700. For a subsidized loan, you would enter repayment owing just the original $10,000. For both subsidized and unsubsidized loans, you will accrue interest while you gradually pay down the debt.

    To save money on the cost of student loans, you can choose from four strategies:

    • borrow less
    • shop around for lower interest rates (this will take research and additional loan applications)
    • pay down interest on unsubsidized debt before repayment begins (to avoid capitalization—interest on interest)
    • make bigger monthly payments to repay your loans faster.

    As this graph shows, someone with about $30,000 in student loans (typical for graduates with bachelor’s degrees) will save more than $10,000 in interest by repaying their loan in 10 years instead of 25 years.

    Potential benefits and risks

    So why take out student loans? A degree can open up more career options and increase your future earnings. Successfully repaying your student loans can help build your credit history. If higher education helps you reach your goals, the cost of student loans may be a good investment in your future—if you can afford to repay them.

    But be aware of the financial tradeoffs you’ll face when you leave school and enter repayment:

    • You will owe monthly payments for many years. The average repayment period for the typical amount of debt is 20 years. These payments will take up money in your budget that you won’t be able to spend on other things.
    • Missing payments can keep you from other goals. If you miss a payment deadline, your credit score might go down. Lower credit scores can make credit cards, home mortgages, and auto loans harder to get approved for and more expensive due to higher interest rates. (If you’re having trouble affording your payments, contact your servicer. Federal loans offer income-driven repayment plans.)
    • Repaying student loans is the only way you’re guaranteed to get rid of them. While loan forgiveness programs do exist for federal loans, they’re not guaranteed. It is very difficult to get rid of student loans in bankruptcy, and you should not rely on that option.

    Other loan options

    If you still have uncovered costs after working with the financial aid office to use up all the options listed above, you and your family have two more loan options to consider:

    • Federal parent PLUS loan : For dependent students at participating schools, parents and stepparents may be able to apply for a parent PLUS loan from the U.S. Department of Education. Check with the financial aid office before applying for this loan.
    • Private student loan: You or a family member can apply for a private student loan from a bank or credit union. Students without credit history are typically required to have a family member cosign as a fellow borrower on the loan.
    {{ svg_icon('warning-round') }}
    Know before you go
    • Accessibility: Parent PLUS Loans are typically easier to qualify for than private loans. All borrowers can save money by comparison shopping—apply to multiple lenders to get the best terms.
    • Cost: For borrowers and cosigners with strong credit, private loans may offer lower interest rates than parent PLUS loans— but watch out for variable interest rates, which can go up in the future. Any borrower can save money on interest by starting repayment as soon as possible.
    • Protections: Parent PLUS loans offer some, but not all, of the protections as Federal Direct Loans to students. Private loans are not legally required to offer the same borrower protections and repayment options as any federal loans.
    • Risk: With both types of loans, borrowers and cosigners risk harm to their credit score if the debt is not repaid on time. Defaulting on a federal loan can lead to garnishment of federal tax refunds and Social Security payments. Parents and other family members should consider whether they have saved enough for emergencies and retirement when deciding whether to borrow money for their student’s education.
    • Further guidance: Visit the U.S. Department of Education for a detailed discussion of loan options. You can also contact the school’s financial aid office.

    How to put this plan into action

    1. Ask the financial aid office any questions you still have.

    It’s first on this list, but you should do it whenever you’re confused or unsure of what to do next. They want to help you (and it’s their job)! You will not jeopardize your funding by asking too many questions.

    2. Find out what it takes to enroll.

    How much is the deposit? What paperwork do you need to send in? When are the money and paperwork due?

    3. Research the details of your grants and scholarships.

    Read or ask about the requirements of each grant and scholarship.

    4. Make a budget.

    Use this cashflow budget to plan out when you’ll get money and when you’ll have to pay it.

    5. Double check that you’ll have enough money when you need it.

    If you have uncovered direct costs, talk to the financial aid office about options like tuition installment plans. Or can you (or your family) start saving for these now?

    6. Accept the loans you want.

    More paperwork ahead! You’ll also need to do entrance counseling for federal loans. Remember, you don’t need to accept all the loans you’re offered or the full amount.

    7. Know how many credits you already have and make a plan to graduate on time.

    Make sure you get credit for your AP, IB, dual-enrollment, and transfer courses. Work with an academic adviser to stay on track for on-time graduation. If you will start school with more than a few credits, ask if any of your funding this year will be affected if you reach second- or third-year student status.

    8. Ask about getting credits from a more affordable school.

    If you’re going to a 4-year school, reach out to your academic advisor about taking core requirements at community college. Make sure they will transfer and count towards your degree.

    9. If you need them, apply for federal parent PLUS or private student loans.

    To get the best deal for your situation, read the fine print and shop around for the lowest interest rates and fees. You’ll probably need a cosigner for private loans.

    10. Put a reminder in your calendar to fill out FAFSA on October 1.

    If you’ll be in school next year, you’ll need to start this process again in a few months.

    11. Set another reminder to visit the financial aid office every semester.

    Ask about new opportunities for funding. For example, some scholarships are only open to certain majors or only for upperclassmen.

    Consider other options

    1. Ask about getting credits from a more affordable school.

    Reach out to your academic advisor about options like taking core requirements at community college.

    2. Reduce your indirect costs.

    Can you live at home? Stick to public transit?

    3. Look for more grants and scholarships.

    There are always more available, even if school starts in a week or two! Scholarship Finder is one place to look

    4. Consider working, if you weren’t going to.

    Remember, up to 20 hours a week is associated with better grades.

    5. Think about other programs at this school.

    Choose a major that will open the door to more lucrative career fields.

    6. Explore other schools.

    Even if this is the only school you applied to, it’s not your only option. For example, if you are pursuing a bachelor’s degree, you can start at community college this year and plan to apply for a transfer next year.
