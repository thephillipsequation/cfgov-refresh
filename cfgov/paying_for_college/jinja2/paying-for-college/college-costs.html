    How do student loans work?

    Loans are money you have to pay back with interest. That means you'll pay more than you originally borrowed. To save money on the cost of student loans, you have three options: borrow less, borrow at a lower interest rate, or take less time to repay your loans by making bigger monthly payments. (As the graph below shows, you’ll pay far more interest over 25 years than over 10.)

    So why take out student loans? A degree can open up more career options and increase your future earnings. If higher education helps you reach your goals, the cost of student loans may be a good investment in your future—if you can afford to repay them.

    But be aware of the risks and tradeoffs:

    • You will owe monthly payments for many years. The average repayment period for the typical amount of debt is 20 years. These payments will take up money in your budget that you won’t be able to spend on other things.
    • Missing payments can keep you from other goals. If you can’t afford your payments and miss a payment deadline, your credit score might go down. Lower credit scores can make it harder to get approved for a credit card or borrow money to buy a car or house. Lower credit scores can also result in higher interest rates and make borrowing money later more expensive.
    • Repaying student loans is the only way you’re guaranteed to get rid of them. While loan forgiveness programs do exist for federal loans, it's not guaranteed. Even declaring bankruptcy usually doesn't dissolve student debt.

    What do I do next?

    Your next steps depend on whether you want to use this plan to pay for attending this school.

    Put this plan into action

    1. Ask the financial aid office any questions you still have.

    It’s first on this list, but you can should do it whenever you’re confused or unsure of what to do next.

    2. Find out what it takes to enroll.

    How much is the deposit? What paperwork do you need to send in? When are the money and paperwork due?

    3. Research the details of your grants and scholarships.

    Read or ask about the requirements of each grant and scholarship.

    4. Double check that you’ll have enough money when you need it.

    If you have uncovered upfront costs, talk to the financial aid office about options like tuition installment plans. Explore savings options, it is never too late to start. Or explore whether your family is able to contribute.

    5. Accept the loans you want.

    More paperwork! You’ll also need to do entrance counseling online for federal loans. Remember, you don’t need to accept all the loans, ask your financial aid office to reduce your loans amounts if you would like to borrow less.

    6. Apply for additional institutional or private student loans.

    To get the best deal for your situation, read the fine print and shop around for the lowest interest rates and fees. You’ll probably need a cosigner.

    7. Make a budget.

    Use this cashflow budget to plan out when you’ll get money and when you’ll have to pay it.

    8. Put a reminder in your calendar to fill out FAFSA on October 1.

    If you’ll be in school next year, you’ll need to start this process again in a few months.

    9. Set another reminder to visit the financial aid office every semester.

    Ask about new opportunities for funding. For example, some scholarships are only open to certain majors or only for upperclassmen.

    10. Ask about getting credits from a more affordable school.

    Reach out to your academic advisor about taking core requirements at community college.

    Consider other options

    1. Ask about getting credits from a more affordable school.

    Reach out to your academic advisor about taking core requirements at community college.

    2. Reduce your living costs.

    Can you live at home? Stick to public transit?

    3. Look for more grants and scholarships.

    There are always more available, even if school starts in a week or two!

    4. Consider working, if you weren’t going to.

    Remember, up to 20 hours a week is associated with better grades.

    5. Think about other programs at this school.

    6. Explore other schools.

    Even if this is the only school you applied to, it’s not your only option. You can always start at community college and plan to apply for a transfer next year.
